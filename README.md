## Project Overview
 
This repository contains data, analysis scripts, and visualisations for a URSS-funded fieldwork project examining the political economy of far-right and populist electoral support in the former German Democratic Republic. The project focuses on the legacy of post-reunification privatisation and deindustrialisation via the Treuhandanstalt, and its relationship to electoral mobilisation by the AfD, BSW, and NPD.
 
A core methodological component is rhetorical analysis of Bundestag *Plenarprotokolle* across multiple legislative periods, using debates on antisemitism as a lens through which party ideological positions, definitional contestation, and coalitional dynamics can be systematically compared.

## Plenarprotokoll Analysis — Antisemitism Debates
 
### Sessions Analysed
 
| Session | Date | Motion | Drucksache |
|---|---|---|---|
| 20/197 | 7 November 2024 | *Nie wieder ist jetzt — Jüdisches Leben in Deutschland schützen, bewahren und stärken* | 20/13627 |
| 20/209 | 29 January 2025 | *Antisemitismus und Israelfeindlichkeit an Schulen und Hochschulen entschlossen entgegentreten sowie den freien Diskursraum sichern* | 20/14703 |
 
---
 
### Voting Patterns
 
Both motions were jointly tabled by SPD, CDU/CSU, Bündnis 90/Die Grünen, and FDP. The vote structure was identical across both sessions:
 
| Party | Vote (20/197) | Vote (20/209) |
|---|---|---|
| SPD | Yes (with dissent) | Yes |
| CDU/CSU | Yes | Yes |
| Bündnis 90/Die Grünen | Yes (with abstentions) | Yes |
| FDP | Yes | Yes |
| AfD | Yes (instrumentalising) | Yes (instrumentalising) |
| BSW | No | No |
| Die Linke | Abstained | Abstained |
 
The stability of this coalition structure across two months and two distinct thematic framings indicates that the cleavage is structural rather than issue-specific. It maps onto disagreements over the IHRA definition of antisemitism and the inclusion of language attributing antisemitism to immigration from North Africa and the Near and Middle East.
 
---
 
### Party Positions
 
#### SPD
The SPD grounded both speeches in the language of historical responsibility, invoking the 80th anniversary of the liberation of Auschwitz in session 20/209 and the upcoming *Reichspogromnacht* anniversary in 20/197. All speakers affirmed Israel's right to exist as *Staatsräson* and named antisemitism from the right, left, and Islamist movements as equally serious threats. In 20/197, several SPD members filed formal dissenting statements (§31 GO) objecting to the passage attributing antisemitism specifically to immigration from certain regions, which they characterised as stigmatising. By 20/209, no such dissent arose, suggesting the more narrowly scoped framing of the schools-and-universities motion reduced internal friction.
 
#### CDU/CSU
The CDU/CSU's emphasis shifted notably between the two sessions. In 20/197, speakers prominently attacked the then-Vice President Aydan Özoğuz by name, calling for her removal, and foregrounded the need to tighten asylum, residence, and criminal law as responses to antisemitism. By 20/209, the focus was more institutional: speakers cited specific incidents at the Humboldt University, TU Berlin, and Alice Salomon Hochschule, and called for professional antisemitism commissioners and structured dialogue between universities and security services. The CDU/CSU consistently took the most assertive position on the IHRA definition, treating any alternative as dishonest.
 
#### Bündnis 90/Die Grünen
The Greens were the most internally divided party in 20/197: seven members filed §31 statements, some abstaining, over concerns that the migration passage risked fuelling anti-Muslim racism. In 20/209 the party was fully united; Kai Gehring's speech balanced the motion's substance with a pointed condemnation of the CDU/CSU's decision, on the same evening, to pass a migration motion with AfD support — calling it a *schäbige Zäsur*. The Greens also took the most data-driven position on empirical claims, citing studies showing universities are no more antisemitic than other social institutions.
 
#### FDP
The FDP gave the most consistently pro-IHRA and pro-Israel speeches across both sessions, citing Israel as "the only democracy in the Middle East" and explicitly opposing the academic boycott of Israeli institutions. In 20/197, Ria Schröder and Linda Teuteberg both quoted Jean Améry's concept of *ehrbarerAntisemitismus* to frame Israel-related antisemitism as the predominant and most socially acceptable form. In 20/209, the FDP was one of two parties (alongside CDU/CSU) to explicitly oppose the BDS movement and call for scientific collaboration with Israel.
 
#### AfD
The AfD's rhetorical strategy was identical across both sessions and constitutes a pattern worth analysing in detail. In each case the party voted for the motion while using its floor time almost entirely to:
 
1. Claim the motion vindicated positions the AfD had long held (particularly on Islam and immigration as primary drivers of antisemitism).
2. Characterise all other parties as hypocrites belatedly adopting AfD language.
3. Call for the deportation of Muslim antisemites rather than educational or preventive measures.
4. Dismiss the motion as "empty words" given the other parties' record.
Critically, the AfD's framing systematically downplayed right-wing extremist antisemitism: in 20/197 Jürgen Braun barely mentioned it; in 20/209 Michael Kaufmann acknowledged "some" right-wing antisemites existed but called it marginal. The Halle synagogue attack of October 2019 was not mentioned by the AfD in either debate. This constitutes what scholars term *definitional capture*: appropriating the language of anti-antisemitism while redirecting its political content.
 
#### BSW
BSW voted against both motions on consistent grounds: the IHRA definition is scientifically contested, its application to funding decisions constitutes state interference in academic and cultural freedom, and legitimate criticism of Israeli government policy risks being criminalised. In 20/197, Sevim Dağdelen's §31 statement cited jurists including Ralf Michaels (Max Planck Institute) and former constitutional court judge Andreas Paulus. By 20/209, Andrej Hunko cited the Hochschulrektorenkonferenz resolution of November 2024 and historian Ulrich Herbert, and explicitly named the Jerusalem Declaration on Antisemitism (2021) as a more precise alternative definition.
 
#### Die Linke
Die Linke abstained in both sessions. In 20/197, Gregor Gysi's speech gave the most historically grounded account of antisemitism's origins, tracing it to Christian anti-Judaism, but the party's §31 statement (Reichinnek, Pellmann) criticised the motion's failure to mention the Halle attack, its relative neglect of right-wing antisemitism compared to immigration-linked antisemitism, and the IHRA definition's legal risks. In 20/209, Nicole Gohlke characterised the motion as a *Schaufensterantrag* — a shop-window motion without funding — and was the only speaker to frame university-police cooperation as itself a threat to academic freedom rather than a solution.

### Key Analytical Findings
 
**1. The definition question is the real fault line.**
All parties support combating antisemitism in principle. The cleavage is over what antisemitism *is*. The IHRA Working Definition (adopted by over 40 countries) includes several examples relating to Israel, which BSW, Linke, and dissenters within SPD and Greens argue risk conflating legitimate political criticism with hatred. CDU/CSU and FDP treat any such argument as bad-faith obfuscation. This is not a new debate: the Bundestagsbeschluss of 2019 and subsequent litigation (BVerwG 2022) already established that applying the IHRA definition to public funding decisions can be unconstitutional.
 
**2. The AfD's strategy is cross-session consistent and analytically distinctive.**
The AfD's pattern of supporting anti-antisemitism motions whilst redirecting their content towards an immigration-and-Islam frame is consistent across both sessions analysed. This is directly relevant to the project's broader question about the AfD's rhetorical construction of identity in the former GDR, where post-reunification grievances (Treuhand, deindustrialisation, wage gaps) intersect with ethno-nationalist cultural framing.
 
**3. Internal dissent in 20/197 but not 20/209.**
The 13+ §31 statements in 20/197 — primarily from SPD and Greens — reflected discomfort with the motion's scope (general protection of Jewish life with Israel policy implications) and its migration language. The schools-and-universities framing of 20/209 was narrower and less contested, producing no formal dissent. This suggests the breadth of the November motion, not antisemitism policy per se, generated internal conflict.
 
**4. BSW's position is more intellectually substantive than its parliamentary marginalisation suggests.**
In both debates, BSW cited specific legal experts, academic texts, and international documents (Jerusalem Declaration, Hochschulrektorenkonferenz). This is atypical of parliamentary dissent and merits attention for the project's analysis of how BSW positions itself relative to the AfD in eastern Germany.

## Methodology
 
### PDF Extraction
Bundestag *Plenarprotokolle* are published as tagged PDFs at `bundestag.de`. Text extraction uses `pdftotext` with layout preservation, followed by speaker-turn segmentation by party affiliation. See `scripts/extract_plenarprotokoll.py`.
 
### Rhetorical Coding
Speeches are coded on five dimensions (0–3 scale):
 
1. **Rechtsextremismus** — emphasis on right-wing extremist antisemitism
2. **Islamist./immigrierter Antisemitismus** — emphasis on Islamist or immigration-linked antisemitism
3. **Wissenschaftsfreiheit/Definition** — engagement with definitional contestation and academic freedom
4. **Bildung/Erinnerungskultur** — emphasis on education and memory culture
5. **Israel/Staatsräson** — foregrounding of Israel's right to exist and German *Staatsräson*
Coding is done at the faction level by aggregating individual speeches. See `docs/coding_scheme.md` for full inter-rater reliability protocol.
